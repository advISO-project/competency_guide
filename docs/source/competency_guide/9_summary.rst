=======
Summary
=======

This guide has set out a practical approach to defining, assessing, and
maintaining bioinformatics competency in a way that is compatible with
ISO 15189 and ISO 17025. This page draws together the key points from each
section as a quick reference.

Competency requirements of the ISO standards
-----------------------------------------------

Both ISO 15189 and ISO 17025 treat competence of personnel as a central
requirement, applying equally to wet-lab and computational work. Three
core requirements apply across both standards: personnel must only carry
out tasks they have been trained, assessed, and authorised for; competency
must be assessed and documented through traceable records (training,
assessment outcomes, authorisation status, and periodic reassessment); and
competency must be actively maintained over time through ongoing training
and reassessment rather than treated as a one-off achievement.

Limitations of Direct Observations of Practice for bioinformatics
---------------------------------------------------------------------

Direct Observations of Practice (DOPs) are a well-established and valid
assessment method under both ISO standards, but they only suit procedural,
observable tasks with a fixed set of steps. Most bioinformatics work does not 
share these characteristics: pipeline development and analysis is iterative, 
relies on cognitive and diagnostic reasoning, allows multiple valid approaches 
to the same problem, and involves decision-making that is largely invisible to 
an observer. Applying DOPs beyond their appropriate scope risks assessing
superficial behaviour rather than genuine competence.

Competency frameworks: an alternative for bioinformatics
------------------------------------------------------------

Competency frameworks offer a structured, realistic way to capture skills
that are not easily observed through a single standardised procedure. They are
particularly suited to bioinformatics, where work is analytical, iterative,
and varies significantly between projects.

A bioinformatics skills framework for pathogen genomics
-------------------------------------------------------------

We set out a bioinformatics skills framework tailored to pathogen genomics
but easily adaptable to other clinical bioinformatics domains, such as
rare-disease or cancer genomics. Adapted from the framework used by Public
Health Wales, itself based on the CDC's bioinformatics competency
framework, it describes eight competency areas covering the responsibilities
involved in bioinformatics work: biology and computer science knowledge, 
statistical methods, biological data analysis, data management, domain-specific 
expertise, system administration, workflow engineering and software development, 
and quality management.

Assessing competency using portfolios
------------------------------------------

A portfolio-based approach is proposed as the primary method for evidencing
competency, adapted from established practice in programmes such as the NHS
Scientist Training Programme. Staff self-assess their level, gather
supporting evidence — analysis outputs, code and workflows, documentation,
presentations, quality activities, or reflective statements — and submit it
for review and sign-off by a senior team member. A dedicated template
supports both individual evidence records and team-wide tracking of
assessment status, including validity and reassessment timeframes.

Mapping competencies to Standard Operating Procedures (SOPs)
------------------------------------------------------------------

Portfolio evidence alone does not confirm readiness to perform a specific
SOP, since most SOPs require several competencies at different levels
working together. We recommend mapping each SOP to the minimum competency
levels required, broken down by level of responsibility e.g. monitoring only,
full SOP under supervision, full SOP independently, and troubleshooting, 
so that broad framework competencies translate directly into task-level
authorisation.

Using the competency framework to support training and development
-------------------------------------------------------------------------

Competency profiles are not just a record of current skill — they provide a
basis for identifying development needs, planning training, and
understanding the overall skill mix and resilience of the team. Core
activities include tracking available training courses, identifying skills
gaps, resilience and succession planning, wider workforce planning, and
addressing collective team training needs. Competency records work best
when integrated into existing appraisal and personal development processes.
